1.  *Gimbel v. Signal Cos.*, 316 A.2d 599 (Del. Ch. 1974) (analyzing whether an asset sale constitutes "substantially all" of a corporation's assets under DGCL § 271, requiring shareholder approval).

2.  *Weinberger v. UOP, Inc.*, 457 A.2d 701 (Del. 1983) (modernizing appraisal valuation by rejecting the "Delaware block" method in favor of standard financial valuation techniques; establishing "entire fairness" as the standard of review for controlling shareholder squeeze-out mergers).

3.  *Unocal Corp. v. Mesa Petroleum Co.*, 493 A.2d 946 (Del. 1985) (establishing "enhanced scrutiny" for defensive measures against hostile takeovers; directors must show reasonable grounds for believing a danger to corporate policy exists and that the response is reasonable in relation to the threat posed).

4.  *Revlon, Inc. v. MacAndrews & Forbes Holdings, Inc.*, 506 A.2d 173 (Del. 1986) (holding that when the break-up or sale of the company is inevitable, the board's duty shifts from preserving the corporate entity to maximizing immediate value for shareholders; the "auctioneer" duty).

5.  *Blasius Indus., Inc. v. Atlas Corp.*, 564 A.2d 651 (Del. Ch. 1988) (holding that board actions taken for the primary purpose of interfering with the shareholder franchise require a "compelling justification," a higher standard than *Unocal*).

6.  *Paramount Commc'ns, Inc. v. Time Inc.*, 571 A.2d 1140 (Del. 1989) (holding that a strategic stock-for-stock merger did not trigger *Revlon* duties because control remained in the market; affirming the board's authority to pursue long-term strategy over immediate short-term premiums).

7.  *Paramount Commc'ns Inc. v. QVC Network Inc.*, 637 A.2d 34 (Del. 1994) (clarifying that a sale of control—where a single shareholder moves from fluid market control to a controlling position—triggers *Revlon* enhanced scrutiny, distinguishing it from *Time Warner*).

8.  *Kahn v. Lynch Commc'n Sys., Inc.*, 638 A.2d 1110 (Del. 1994) (holding that entire fairness applies to controller buyouts even with independent committee approval, but the burden of proof shifts if the committee functions effectively; mandated that controllers cannot dictate terms).

9.  *Unitrin, Inc. v. Am. Gen. Corp.*, 651 A.2d 1361 (Del. 1995) (refining *Unocal*; defensive measures must not be "coercive or preclusive" and must fall within a "range of reasonableness").

10. *Omnicare, Inc. v. NCS Healthcare, Inc.*, 818 A.2d 914 (Del. 2003) (holding that deal protection devices [voting agreements + force-the-vote provisions] that make a merger a *fait accompli* are preclusive and invalid under *Unocal*; the board must retain a "fiduciary out").

11. *Lyondell Chem. Co. v. Ryan*, 970 A.2d 235 (Del. 2009) (clarifying that *Revlon* does not require a specific set of steps [like an auction] but requires the board to act reasonably to secure the best price; speed alone does not equal a breach of duty).

12. *Air Prods. & Chems., Inc. v. Airgas, Inc.*, 16 A.3d 48 (Del. Ch. 2011) (upholding a board's use of a poison pill to block a non-coercive but inadequate all-cash offer, effectively allowing the board to "just say no" indefinitely if acting in good faith).

13. *Kahn v. M&F Worldwide Corp.*, 88 A.3d 635 (Del. 2014) (establishing the "MFW" framework: business judgment review applies to controller freeze-outs if conditioned *ab initio* on both an independent special committee and a majority-of-the-minority vote).

14. *Corwin v. KKR Fin. Holdings LLC*, 125 A.3d 304 (Del. 2015) (holding that a fully informed, uncoerced vote of disinterested stockholders cleanses most fiduciary breaches and invokes the business judgment rule, leading to dismissal).

15. *C & J Energy Servs., Inc. v. City of Miami Gen. Emps.' & Sanitation Emps.' Ret. Tr.*, 107 A.3d 1049 (Del. 2014) (reversing a mandatory injunction requiring an auction; emphasizing that *Revlon* is a standard of review, not a rigid prescription for how directors must act).

16. *Dell, Inc. v. Magnetar Glob. Event Driven Master Fund Ltd.*,  Below I (Del. 2017) (holding that the deal price in a robust, arm's-length process is often the best evidence of "fair value" in appraisal proceedings, undermining appraisal arbitrage strategies).

17. *DFC Glob. Corp. v. Muirfield Value Partners, L.P.*, 172 A.3d 346 (Del. 2017) (reinforcing that market price is the gold standard for valuation in appraisal cases when the market is efficient).

18. *Verition Partners Master Fund Ltd. v. Aruba Networks, Inc.*, 210 A.3d 128 (Del. 2019) (calculating "fair value" as the deal price minus synergies; further restricting appraisal arbitrage).

19. *In re Match Group, Inc. Derivative Litig.*, 315 A.3d 446 (Del. 2024) (holding that to obtain business judgment review under *MFW*, a controlling stockholder transaction must employ *both* procedural protections—independent committee and minority vote—regardless of whether it is a squeeze-out merger or another type of conflict transaction).
