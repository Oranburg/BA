# **Air Products and Chemicals, Inc. v. Airgas, Inc.**

**Court of Chancery of Delaware**

February 15, 2011

16 A.3d 48

Chandler, Chancellor:

# **Introduction**

It is now February 2011\. For over a year, Air Products & Chemicals, Inc. has sought to acquire Airgas, Inc. Its initial offer, made privately in October 2009, was for $60 per share. Its current "best and final" offer, made public in December 2010, is for $70 per share. The Airgas board of directors has repeatedly rejected Air Products' offers as inadequate. The board's view is that the company is worth at least $78 per share in a sale transaction. To defend against Air Products' unsolicited offers, the Airgas board has relied on a shareholder rights plan—a "poison pill"—that would be triggered if an acquirer obtained 15% or more of the company's shares.

Air Products has launched a two-pronged attack on Airgas's poison pill. First, it has nominated three independent directors to the Airgas board, who were elected by the stockholders in September 2010\. Second, it has brought this action in the Court of Chancery, seeking a declaratory judgment that the Airgas board has breached its fiduciary duties by maintaining the pill and asking this Court to order the board to redeem the pill.

This case poses the following question: Can a board of directors, acting in good faith and with a reasonable factual basis, maintain a poison pill to block a premium, all-cash, fully financed tender offer that the board believes is inadequate?

For the reasons explained below, I conclude that the answer to this question is yes.

Under Delaware law, the business judgment rule is the "presumption that in making a business decision the directors of a corporation acted on an informed basis, in good faith and in the honest belief that the action taken was in the best interests of the company." When a board adopts or maintains defensive measures in response to a hostile takeover threat, however, the board's actions are subject to the enhanced judicial scrutiny established in *Unocal*.

The *Unocal* test requires the board to demonstrate that: (1) it had reasonable grounds for believing that a danger to corporate policy and effectiveness existed; and (2) its defensive response was reasonable in relation to the threat posed.

In this case, the Airgas board has met its burden under *Unocal*. First, the board has demonstrated that Air Products' offer poses a legally cognizable threat to Airgas. The board believes, in good faith and after a reasonable investigation, that the offer is inadequate. The board also believes that a majority of Airgas's stockholders may tender into the inadequate offer because they have "wearied of the battle" or because they are "short-termers" (arbitrageurs and hedge funds) who are willing to take a quick profit at the expense of long-term value. This type of threat—that stockholders will tender into an inadequate offer—has been recognized by the Delaware Supreme Court as a valid threat in *Unitrin* and *Paramount*.

Second, the board has demonstrated that maintaining the pill is a proportionate response to this threat. The pill is not "coercive" or "preclusive." It is not coercive because it does not force stockholders to accept a management-sponsored alternative. It is not preclusive because it does not render a successful proxy contest a "mathematical impossibility" or "realistically unattainable." Indeed, Air Products recently ran a successful proxy contest, electing three directors to the Airgas board. If Air Products wishes to acquire Airgas, it can do so by running another proxy contest next year and electing a new board majority that is willing to redeem the pill.

Air Products argues that the Airgas board has "just said no" for too long and that this Court should intervene to let the stockholders decide. But Delaware law does not require a board to redeem a poison pill simply because a majority of stockholders want to tender their shares. As the Supreme Court held in *Paramount*, directors are not "obliged to abandon a deliberately conceived corporate plan for a short-term shareholder profit unless there is clearly no basis to sustain the corporate strategy."

The power to manage the business and affairs of a corporation resides with the board of directors, not the stockholders. The stockholders have the power to elect the board, but they do not have the power to dictate business decisions. If the stockholders are unhappy with the board's decision to maintain the pill, their remedy is to replace the board, not to ask this Court to substitute its judgment for that of the directors.

Accordingly, Air Products' request for an order requiring the Airgas board to redeem the poison pill is denied.

# **Facts**

## **A. The Parties**

Plaintiff Air Products & Chemicals, Inc. is a Delaware corporation headquartered in Allentown, Pennsylvania. It serves technology, energy, industrial, and healthcare customers globally with a portfolio of products, services, and solutions that include atmospheric gases, process and specialty gases, performance materials, and equipment and services.

Defendant Airgas, Inc. is a Delaware corporation headquartered in Radnor, Pennsylvania. Airgas is the largest U.S. distributor of industrial, medical, and specialty gases, and hardgoods, such as welding equipment and supplies. Airgas is also a leading U.S. producer of atmospheric gases, carbon dioxide, dry ice, and nitrous oxide, one of the largest U.S. distributors of safety products, and a leading U.S. distributor of refrigerants, ammonia products, and process chemicals.

The individual defendants are the members of the Airgas board of directors.

## **B. Airgas's Anti-Takeover Devices**

Airgas has a classified board, meaning that its directors are divided into three classes, with each class serving a three-year term. This structure is set forth in Airgas's certificate of incorporation.

Airgas also has a shareholder rights plan, commonly known as a "poison pill." The pill was originally adopted in 1996 and has been renewed several times. The current pill was adopted in 2007\. Under the terms of the pill, if a person or group acquires 15% or more of Airgas's outstanding common stock, the pill is triggered, allowing all other stockholders to purchase additional shares of Airgas stock at a substantial discount. This would severely dilute the holdings of the acquiring person, making a hostile takeover prohibitively expensive.

## **C. Airgas's Five-Year Plan**

In November 2009, shortly after receiving Air Products' initial expression of interest, Airgas management presented a five-year strategic plan to the board. The plan projected significant growth in Airgas's earnings and cash flow over the next five years. The board used this plan as the basis for its valuation of the company and its determination that Air Products' offers were inadequate.

## **D. The Battle for Airgas**

### **1\. The $60 All-Cash Offer**

On February 4, 2010, Air Products publicly announced a tender offer to acquire all outstanding shares of Airgas for $60 per share in cash. The Airgas board unanimously rejected this offer as inadequate.

### **2\. The $63.50 Offer**

In July 2010, Air Products raised its offer to $63.50 per share. The Airgas board again unanimously rejected the offer as inadequate.

### **3\. The Proxy Contest**

At Airgas's annual meeting in September 2010, Air Products ran a proxy contest seeking to elect three independent directors to the Airgas board. Air Products' nominees—John Clancey, Robert Lumpkins, and Ted Miller—were elected by a substantial margin. Air Products also sought to pass several bylaw amendments, including a bylaw that would require the annual meeting to be held in January. The validity of that bylaw was the subject of prior litigation in this Court, which held that the bylaw was valid but that the annual meeting would be held in September 2011\.

### **4\. The $65.50 Offer**

In September 2010, Air Products raised its offer to $65.50 per share. The Airgas board, including the three newly elected directors, unanimously rejected this offer as inadequate.

### **5\. The $70 "Best and Final" Offer**

On December 9, 2010, Air Products raised its offer to $70 per share and declared that this was its "best and final" offer. The Airgas board met to consider this offer. The board received advice from its financial advisors, Bank of America Merrill Lynch, Goldman Sachs, and Credit Suisse, all of whom opined that the $70 offer was inadequate from a financial point of view.

The three new directors—Clancey, Lumpkins, and Miller—retained their own independent financial advisor and legal counsel. After conducting their own due diligence and receiving advice from their own advisors, the three new directors joined the rest of the board in unanimously rejecting the $70 offer as inadequate.

The Airgas board expressed its willingness to negotiate with Air Products if Air Products offered a price that reflected the true value of the company, which the board believed was at least $78 per share. Air Products refused to raise its offer above $70.

# **Analysis**

## **A. The Standard of Review**

The proper standard of review for a board's decision to maintain a poison pill in response to a hostile tender offer is the enhanced scrutiny standard set forth in *Unocal Corp. v. Mesa Petroleum Co.*

Under *Unocal*, the directors must show that they had reasonable grounds for believing that a danger to corporate policy and effectiveness existed. They satisfy that burden by showing good faith and reasonable investigation. Second, the directors must show that their defensive response was reasonable in relation to the threat posed.

This intermediate standard of review applies because of the "omnipresent specter that a board may be acting primarily in its own interests, rather than those of the corporation and its shareholders."

## **B. Has the Board Made a Good Faith and Reasonable Investigation?**

The first prong of *Unocal* requires the board to demonstrate that it identified a legitimate threat to corporate policy and effectiveness.

The Airgas board has identified the threat as "substantive coercion"—the risk that stockholders will disbelieve the board's views on value and tender into an inadequate offer. The Delaware Supreme Court recognized this as a valid threat in *Paramount Communications, Inc. v. Time Inc.* and *Unitrin, Inc. v. American General Corp.*

Air Products argues that substantive coercion is not a valid threat in this case because Airgas's stockholders are sophisticated institutions who have all the information they need to make a decision. Air Products contends that the "inadequate price" theory is merely a pretext for entrenchment.

I disagree. The record demonstrates that the Airgas board has acted in good faith and has conducted a reasonable investigation. The board is comprised of a majority of independent directors. It has met numerous times to consider Air Products' offers. It has received advice from three different financial advisors. It has relied on a detailed five-year plan that projects significant future value.

Most importantly, the three directors nominated by Air Products and elected by the stockholders—Clancey, Lumpkins, and Miller—have joined the rest of the board in rejecting the $70 offer. These three directors are indisputably independent. They were elected on Air Products' platform. Yet, after looking at the numbers and fulfilling their fiduciary duties, they concluded that $70 is too low. Their concurrence is powerful evidence that the board is acting in good faith.

Air Products argues that the board is clinging to an unrealistic valuation of $78 per share. But valuation is an art, not a science. The board is entitled to rely on its management and advisors in determining the company's value. The board believes that Airgas has a unique business model and a bright future that is not fully appreciated by the market. This belief is not unreasonable.

The board also fears that short-term arbitrageurs, who now hold a significant percentage of Airgas's stock, will tender into the $70 offer to turn a quick profit, regardless of the company's long-term value. This is exactly the type of threat that *Unocal* was designed to address.

Therefore, I conclude that the Airgas board has satisfied the first prong of *Unocal*.

## **C. Is the Continued Maintenance of Airgas's Defensive Measures Proportionate?**

The second prong of *Unocal* requires the board to demonstrate that its defensive response is proportionate to the threat. A defensive measure is disproportionate if it is "draconian," meaning that it is either "coercive" or "preclusive." If the measure is not draconian, the court must then determine whether it falls within a "range of reasonableness."

### **1\. Is the Pill Coercive?**

A defensive measure is coercive if it is aimed at "cramming down" on management's alternative to a hostile offer. Here, the Airgas board is not trying to force stockholders to accept a management plan. It is simply maintaining the status quo to prevent the sale of the company at an inadequate price. Thus, the pill is not coercive.

### **2\. Is the Pill Preclusive?**

This is the heart of the dispute. Air Products argues that the combination of Airgas's classified board and its poison pill makes a successful takeover "realistically unattainable." Air Products contends that it would take two years to replace a majority of the Airgas board, and that no bidder can afford to wait that long.

Under *Unitrin*, a defensive measure is preclusive if it renders a successful proxy contest a "mathematical impossibility" or "realistically unattainable."

Air Products has already proven that a proxy contest is not impossible. It successfully elected three directors in 2010\. It can run another slate of directors at the next annual meeting in September 2011\. If it succeeds, it will have replaced a majority of the board. The new board would then be free to redeem the pill and accept the offer.

Air Products argues that waiting another year is not a realistic option. But Delaware law recognizes that a classified board structure is a valid legislative choice. The fact that a bidder must win two elections to gain control of a classified board does not make the defense preclusive.

In *Versata Enterprises, Inc. v. Selectica, Inc.*, the Delaware Supreme Court reaffirmed that the "preclusive" standard is a high bar. A measure is not preclusive simply because it makes a takeover difficult or expensive. It must effectively block any possibility of a takeover.

Here, Air Products has a clear path to victory: win the next election. The fact that this path takes time does not render it "realistically unattainable."

### **3\. Is the Pill Within the Range of Reasonableness?**

Since the pill is neither coercive nor preclusive, the final question is whether it falls within a range of reasonableness.

The Airgas board believes that the $70 offer is significantly below the company's intrinsic value. The board is protecting the stockholders from selling their shares at a discount. This is a legitimate corporate purpose.

Air Products argues that "enough is enough." It has been over a year. The stockholders should be allowed to decide.

This argument has a certain appeal. But it is inconsistent with Delaware law. As the Supreme Court stated in *Time*, "directors, while not ignoring the wishes of a majority of shareholders, are not obliged to abandon a deliberately conceived corporate plan for a short-term shareholder profit unless there is clearly no basis to sustain the corporate strategy."

The "just say no" defense is a valid strategy under Delaware law, provided the board acts in good faith and reasonably. The Airgas board has done so. It is not for this Court to second-guess the board's business judgment.

# **Conclusion**

For the foregoing reasons, I find that the Airgas board has met its burden under *Unocal*. The board has demonstrated that Air Products' offer poses a legitimate threat to the company and its stockholders. The board has also demonstrated that maintaining the poison pill is a reasonable and proportionate response to that threat.

Therefore, Air Products' request for declaratory and injunctive relief is **DENIED**. All claims against the defendants are **DISMISSED WITH PREJUDICE**.

**IT IS SO ORDERED.**
